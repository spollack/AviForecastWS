Eastern Sierra avalanche advisory
Posted January 11, 2013 by Sue Burak

The avalanche danger is LOW on sheltered slopes in the Mammoth Lakes Basin. Areas of MODERATE danger exist on upper elevation, wind exposed slopes. Very strong southwest and west winds, combined with 4-6 inches of new snow Wednesday night, created fresh wind drifts and slabs near exposed ridgelines, in gullies, and around other prominent terrain features. Northwest winds Thursday created thin windslabs on easterly aspects above treeline. These slabs are likely to be small, but could be of sufficient size to take you for a ride. On slopes that are sheltered from the wind, the snowpack is generally stable. Continue to evaluate terrain carefully and use safe travel practices.

The avalanche danger is generally LOW in the Rock Creek basin. For those venturing into steep alpine terrain, shallow wind slabs can be expected in wind loaded north to east facing gullies and below ridgetops.Evaluate wind loaded terrain carefully and use caution in steep terrain.



 

Strong southwest winds and widespread snow occurred Wednesday night as a strong cold front passed over the area. Three to 6 inches of new, cold, dry snow accumulated across the Mammoth Basin while Rock Creek picked up 1 to 3 inches. Yesterday gusty northwest and west winds blew snow into eddies along ridgetops and created thin winds slabs on northeast to east facing slopes above treeline in the Mammoth area and in the White Wing and Chicken Wing area. While touring in the Chicken Wing area today, I did not see any recent avalanche activity but there wind slabs below ridges on southeast slopes as well as on northeast slopes.


The main avalanche problem in the Mammoth Basin is wind slabs. Today you will find isolated, thin, and moderately reactive wind slabs along the higher ridges, gullies, and other exposed terrain features. While most of these wind slabs should remain less than a foot in depth and not extend very far away from the ridgelines, some larger wind slabs up to a couple feet in depth could exist in the most heavily wind loaded areas. Human triggering of these newly formed wind slabs will be possible today. As the winds continue to blow from the northwest, additional wind slabs could form on east to south aspects and although they will probably be relatively small, these slabs could easily take you for a ride.

The main avalanche problem in the Rock Creek basin is the buried rain crust that is found up to 10,000 to 10,500 feet. Observations from 9,000 to 10,500 ft in the Rock Creek basin show a weak shallow snowpack with a persistent weak layer that continues to propagate fractures through large weak facets above and below the rain crust. Recent observations above 9,500 ft show two rain crusts separated by weak faceted grains. This crust sandwich is the same rain layer structure that was reacting in tests in mid December in the mid elevations of the Mammoth Lakes Basin, before storms buried the layer under 6 to 7 ft of snow.


A weak system and associated cold front brought scattered snow showers through the higher elevations yesterday. This front has also caused temperatures to plummet and morning lows are near zero degrees in most mountain locations. Partly cloudy skies today with brisk northwest winds will create wind chills well below zero for the day. High temperatures at the 8,000 to 10,000 ft elevations might reach 20F and tonightâ€™s lows could be below zero. Higher elevations will be very cold today with highs in the single digits. Temperatures will be 10 to 20 degrees colder than average until the middle of next week.


-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Please note that the avalanche danger rating in this advisory expires in 24 hours. This advisory is our best interpretation of snow pack conditions and NWS forecasts issued today. Backcountry travelers should be aware that elevation and geographic distinctions are approximate and that a transition zone exists between upper and lower elevations. Avalanches do not happen by accident and most human involvement is a matter of choice not chance. Most avalanche accidents are caused by slab avalanches that are triggered by the victim of member of the victim's party. Even small slides can be dangerous. Always practice route finding skills and carry avalanche rescue gear. Remember that avalanche danger ratings are only general guidelines. Distinctions between geographic areas, elevations, slope aspects and slope angles should be made.
