Payette Avalanche Advisory

Monday, December 22 2014

Created:   12-22-2014 at 5:11 am

Forecaster: Dave Bingaman

We have heads up avalanche conditions throughout the West Central and surrounding mountains today. The danger of natural avalanches has dropped today but human caused avalanches are still likely today, be safe!

Don't forget about our Introduction to Avalanche Class coming up on Jan 2 and 3.  The Friday classroom session is open to snowmobilers and skiers with separate field sessions on Saturday.  For our full education calendar click here.  Brundage Mt and Tamarack Resort are now open, remember if you choose to leave the ski resort boundary you are in the backcountry and should be prepared and equipped for travel and/or rescue in avalanche terrain.  Also, mark your calenders for the Friends annual fundraiser at Salmon River Brewery in McCall, February 21.

Bottom Line

DANGER LEVEL DEFINITIONS

Summary

The avalanche hazard today is Considerable through out the West Central Mountains. The storm over the last few days produced a decent sized natural avalanche cycle with crown depths between 1-3 feet. While the hazard of natural avalanches has decreased overnight, human caused avalanches are still likely today on many aspects.  Winds have increased the load in many areas making deeper avalanches possible on loaded slopes.  Brittle wind slabs may be encountered near ridge tops or on any exposed upper elevation terrain.

Statistically more people in Idaho trigger fatal avalanches during periods of Considerable Hazard than at higher levels. Considerable Hazard means that Dangerous avalanche conditions exist on many slopes and that human triggered avalanches are very likely. These conditions require careful snowpack and terrain evaluation and conservative decision making.  If you don't have the skills to make informed decisions in hazardous avalanche terrain then you should not be there today.

The mountains received 25-30 inches of heavy snow over the weekend. In terms of water, this storm was a big one, with local snotel sites gaining between 3 and 4 inches of Snow Water Equivalent in 3 days.  The storm also came in with gusty S winds that eventually shifted to the W and NW as various waves passed through. The most notable factor with this storm was that it began with light and cool snow last week and ended with some heavy wet glop that left us with a significantly inverted snowpack. In addition, the variable winds left a variety of wind slabs and wind deposited snow on multiple aspects increasing the potential for deeper avalanches on these slopes.

We experienced a widespread natural avalanche cycle over the last 2 days with our local ski patrols getting good sized results with explosives and ski cuts at both ends of the valley yesterday.  We also had a report of at least one skier triggered avalanche near Mores Creek Summit with a crown height of 26 inches.  Most of the slides were running within the storm slab while some of the deeper slides may have been initiating in one of the buried weak layers left over from the variable weather that we have had through the last month. The potential for human caused avalanches in the storm snow, wind slab or on one of the deeper persistent weak layers is going to remain very likely over the next few days. Low temperatures in the teens will help the snowpack heal for the next few days but we have another series of windy storms lined up for mid-week. Don't be lulled into a false sense of security right now, we still have plenty of avalanche dragons lurking in our mountains.

Variable winds with speeds reaching into the 30mph range have been affecting the West Central Mountains for the last few days.  These winds had almost 3 feet of new snow to move around increasing the potential depth of avalanches on wind loaded slopes by an exponential amount. Watch for these areas today on SE, E and NE terrain.  Remember that variable winds do a good job top loading ridge top areas but also cross loading many micro features mid slope as well.  Look for visual clues such as sculpted, pillowy or rounded areas and pay attention to changes in the feel or sound of the snow as you travel in wind exposed terrain. If you feel areas of very firm or hollow feeling snow or experience cracking of the snowpack as you move through it, you are on wind slab and should get off of it asap.

Recent Avalanche Activity:

Widespread natural activity reported with small to medium sized avalanches in the D1-D2 size.  Ski patrols at both Brundage and Tamarack reported significant activity in their avalanche mitigation efforts yesterday with 1-2 foot crowns in many areas and larger 2-4 foot crowns reported on the mostly East facing wind loaded terrain at the top of the West Mountain ridgeline. We had 2 separate (?) reports of a good sized D2+ skier triggered avalanche in the Boise Mountains near Mores Creek Summit as well.

Current Conditions

Expect to find a mixed bag of conditions with the inverted snowpack making for some challenging travel on skis or sleds today.  Higher, sheltered terrain is going to be your best bet for finding the soft stuff but avoid the steeps and let the snowpack settle down for a few days.
