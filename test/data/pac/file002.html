Payette Avalanche Advisory

Sunday, December 21 2014

Created:   12-21-2014 at 4:43 am
Updated:  12-21-2014 at 5:09 am

Forecaster: Dave Bingaman

Happy Solstice Day! We have heads up avalanches conditions today, be safe! Don't forget about our Introduction to Avalanche Class coming up on Jan 2 and 3.  The Friday classroom session is open to snowmobilers and skiers with separate field sessions on Saturday.  For our full education calendar click here.  Brundage Mt and Tamarack Resort are now open, remember if you choose to leave the ski resort boundary you are in the backcountry and should be prepared and equipped for travel and/or rescue in avalanche terrain.  Also, mark your calenders for the Friends annual fundraiser at Salmon River Brewery in McCall, February 21. 

Bottom Line

DANGER LEVEL DEFINITIONS

Summary

The avalanche hazard throughout our local mountains will be HIGH today. Natural and human caused avalanches are likely throughout the day as temperatures continue to rise. Avoid travel on backcountry slopes over 30 degrees and be aware of avalanche run out zones and other avalanche terrain around you as you travel through the mountains.

Most of our local mountain areas picked up another 8-15 inches of heavy snow last night further overloading our snowpack.  Strong SW winds shifting to W/NW also played a factor through the night creating additional wind slabs and loading N, NE and E slopes with additional wind deposited snow.

If you are searching for powder today, stick to low angle slopes, keep your eyes on your riding/skiing partners at all times and make sure everyone in your group is carrying the appropriate avalanche rescue gear.

Our mountain snowpack has nearly doubled in the last few days. You don't need to be an avalanche forecaster to realize that puts a lot of strain on the snowpack. Over the last 48 hours, the snowpack has had to adjust to a lot of new weight and has likely already begun to fail in the form of natural avalanches overnight.  There is also an increased risk today of human caused avalanches.  Weak layers that were shallow Friday are now sitting closer to 2 feet down in the snowpack.  The additional weight may also be enough to trigger some of the deeper layers associated with the weak snow at the bottom of our snowpack. In addition, avalanches triggered in the upper portion of the snowpack may now have the ability to step down into these deeper layers creating very large and dense avalanches.

Local snotel sites show 1.5 to 2 inches of additional Snow Water Equivalent in the last 24 hours. The Brundage Mt webcam shows 15 inches at mid mountain this morning with closer to 9 inches at Tamarack Resort. Many avalanche factors are present right now, we call them RED FLAGS. Here is a short video from Recco and TGR describing Red Flag problems, see how many you can pick out based on our recent weather.

The size of our avalanche problem has increased significantly in a short time, use good travel protocols and stick to low angle terrain, don't become a statistic today.

SW winds have been hitting the West Central Mountains for several days creating wind slabs of varying thicknesses.  These winds have also been transporting snow across the upper elevation terrain and filling in the northerly aspects. The addition of new snow on top of these slabs not only adds depth to the problem but also does a great job camoflaging older wind slabs.  The wind has also been crossloading other aspects, mostly on Easterly slopes.  Last night winds gusting into the 30mph range shifted around to the W and NW which will increase the potential for wind slabs and wind loading today on NW, W, SE and E facing slopes. Avoid areas mid slope like gullies or depressions that have been crossloaded.  Today is also a great day to avoid N and NE slopes altogether and watch for hollow sounding or feeling snow, shooting cracks and new cornice formation in any wind affected terrain.

Recent Avalanche Activity:

None reported yet, a partial clearing on Monday may be a good time to look around.  If you see or trigger avalanche activity please send us an email with details. Take a picture if possible or just let us know what aspect and elevation as well as how deep the avalanche was.  You can send us your observations at: forecast@payetteavalanche.org, click on the observations page of our website or call and leave a message at 208-634-0400.

Current Conditions

Deep and getting deeper today with another 4-8 inches possible today.
